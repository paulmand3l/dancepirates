The 90-10 Rule of Close Embrace 1

by Paul Mandel

Emphasize consent throughout. Close embrace is a touchy subject (no pun intended), so encourage students to focus on being receptive and respectful.

Exercise

Remember in that movie Hitch with Will Smith? He plays a guy who teaches other guys how to be more confident with women. He has one lesson in particular that’s well suited to getting into close embrace: the 90-10 rule.

Hitch presents it in the context of kissing at the end of a first date. Whoever is initiating the kiss can only go 90% of the way. You can’t actually kiss them. You ask a question by going that 90%, and they have to answer with the remaining 10%.

We can think about close embrace the same way. When you’re dancing with somebody, and you want to go into close embrace, you should only go 90% of the way to close embrace. Don’t just pull them in. If they want to go into close embrace, they’ll answer with the remaining 10%. If they don’t answer or move back a little bit, go back to closed or whatever you were doing before.

Have students practice this idea. Let both leads and follows practice asking for close embrace, and practice saying both ‘yes’ and ‘no’ and feeling the difference between the two responses.

Discuss consent. Talk about the reasons somebody might not want to dance in close embrace (self conscious, injury, don’t want to get you sweaty, uncomfortable) and encourage students to not take it personally.

  • Just because somebody has said ‘yes’ to close embrace with somebody else, doesn’t mean you should expect them to say ‘yes’ to you.
  • Just because somebody has said ‘yes’ to you before in the past, doesn’t mean you should expect them to say ‘yes’ to you.
  • Just because they’re currently saying ‘yes’ doesn’t mean you should expect them not to change their mind.
