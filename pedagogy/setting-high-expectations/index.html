The Importance of Setting High Expecations

As instructors, we want our students to accomplish as much as possible in our classes. One tactic some instructors employ to acheive this outcome is to set very high expectations for students.

The exact level of the expectation can vary somewhat from “probably acheivable” to “absolutely impossible”. It’s also always a bit of a guessing game, since you never actually know quite what students are capable of. But often, you’ll be surprised at how high your students will rise to meet your expectations.

Make expectations psychological, not physiological

High expectations are particularly effective (especially at first) in driving student success when the blockers are mostly psychological, not physiological. That is, when the problems are not about movement precision or artistic interpretation, but that there is some kind of mental block or lack of discipline that is holding students back.

The problem with setting high expectations is that students can become frustrated and give up if they aren’t (or think they can’t) meet your expecations. This is why I tend to set psychological instead of physiological expectations—it’s generally easier for students to change their behavior, but hard to master a new topic.

So, don’t set expectations for how quickly students progress, but do set expectations on how hard and how much students practice.

Use expectations to establish an effective teacher/student relationship

Take a moment (or several) at the beginning of class to set my expectations for student behavior. Teaching and learning is a professional working relationship, so tell your students what actions and behaviors you need from them to make that relationship as effective as possible.

Avoid the perception of talking down to your students when setting these expectations. Feeling like they are being treated like children lowers their perception of the expectations you’re setting for them.
