Call and Response

It’s useful to be able to confirm that students have actually learned something, and to encourage memorization of certain concepts. To do this, you can employ call and response.

To make this work, you need to deeply and closely associate a call phrase and a response phrase. There are a couple ways to do this.

  1. Very explicitly tell students what the call and response are. Effective, but a little heavy-handed and connects the ideas in a very shallow way.

When I say …, you say …

  1. Say the call and response together a few times. Use as close to the same phrasing each time as you can, and say it with a very slightly singsongy tone.

Rule 0 is to always smile. When you smile at your partner, they like you more and have more fun. So, don’t forget, rule 0 is to smile.

Immediately after establishing the call and response, use it. Say/ask the call phrase, then lean forward a bit and make a ‘your turn’ gesture to the students. Encourage the students who respond.

What’s rule 0? [Always smile!] Good!

Use the call and response a few times over the course of the lesson, and once right at the end to cement it. If you’re doing a series, bring it back in later weeks.

Examples

Before class auditions at Blues Muse, Carsie and the gang used a call and response to get participants engaged and relaxed.

What are we doing? Dancing! Why are we doing it? Just for fun!

She used the explicit form since it was such large group and it was getting students to say aloud and reinforce something they already knew. It worked fantastically well, and students were much less nervous going into auditions.
